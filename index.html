Integrating cycling and walkability

in a land-use and transport model of the Greater Boston Area.

  • Gist
  • Structure
  • Info

Land-use and transport (LUT) models have been developped for forecasting the travel demand of a large metropolitan area. With the famous four-step model, we have been able to produce interesting and reliable results in terms of forecasts. This project aims at broaden the usual framework of a transport model by integrating the bicycle mode, often considered as insignificant or useless, since bicycle will not generate congestion issues before decades. Nevertheless, integrating bicycle, and more generally considering the main active modes (walk and bicycle) in a land-use and transport model might be interesting if we want to explore the impact of the built environment on the active transportation demand.

In this report, we will try to demonstrate that the urban conditions play a more or less critical role on the human-powered mobility demand and that a LUT model can capture it. The report is structured as follows. First, we conducted a large literature review, focusing on bicycle models and how did people tried to operationalize the perception of the facilities, or the safety for example. Second, we conducted a Hubway data analysis, the bicycle sharing system of Boston, in order to understand who are the cyclists, and what conditions make them ride. Third, we developped the mode choice model based on the Massachusetts travel Survey 2010 (MTS), large statewide survey that provided some information about the cyclists. And finally, we were able to start feeding the LUT (integated in the commercial software CUBE LAND and CUBE VOYAGER) with the new mode choice model and play with the model to understand how the city would respond to higher walkalbility or higher bicycle convenience on the road network.

This project has been conducted at MIT in the Dpt. of Urban Studies and Planning (DUSP), under the helpfull supervision of Pr. Chris Zegras, for completing the MSc degree in Civil Engineering at EPFL, Switzerland, with the administrative supervison of Pr. Michel Bierlaire.
